# Vision

**Music Forest is a tool for attention, not consumption.** It helps you *notice,
reflect on, and connect through* music — one album at a time. You bring the
attention; it holds what you find.

And you're not limited to what a given day surfaces. You can reach for any record in
the catalog — any album, artist, or label — and note whatever you find, not only
what today brought you. You go looking; the notebook holds it.

This is the north star: the durable values and the standing no's. It governs *what
we build and won't build* — not how it looks or reads. New
features earn their place by deepening these principles, not by adding surface area.

## Principles

**1. Story over metadata.** What attracts people is the *why* and the human story
behind a record — not catalog numbers. Surface story as *threads to pull*, never
walls of text. Progressive disclosure: a minimal card, a few doors on demand, depth
only when asked.

**2. Connection, in two directions.** The value is the edges between things, not the
album in isolation.
- *Inward* — to whatever the record touches in your own life. What you write is
  yours, readable by you alone, and it's the most valuable thing in the app.
- *Outward* — to the people, scenes, and causes you don't know yet: the record as a
  door to a wider world.

You make these links yourself, in your own words. The app never makes them for you,
and never hands you a set of categories to fill in. It surfaces the *record's* story
— genres, label, the people on it — as threads you can pull; but what *you* write, it
leaves untouched: not mined, not tagged, not built into a picture of you. Your notes
are held as written, and stay private until you look back.

**3. Friction as ritual.** The friction that matters is the *writing*, not the
listening. Playing and keeping records stays easy — we don't ration the music. What
asks a little effort is what makes a record yours: writing a note, making a link
yourself, sitting with it a while. That effort is the reward, the way tending a
garden is — which is why there's no autoplay and no frictionless feed. The only thing
we never make effortless is the noticing, because doing it yourself is the point.

**4. Pull, not push.** The app never resurfaces a person or a memory at you unbidden.
It does not decide a moment is meaningful and serve it back; *you* go looking, on
your terms. Everything the connection layer stores is retrievable **only when you
reach for it**. Its job is to remember faithfully and stay quiet until asked.

**5. Doors, not corridors.** Every outward pull is a *door*: it opens a contained
view over your current context and closes to leave you exactly where you were. Never
a *corridor* — a click that replaces your context, chains on endlessly, and loses
your place. A pull must **(a)** preserve your anchor; **(b)** make going deeper
deliberate and coming back free (one click / Esc); **(c)** open onto something finite
and surveyable; **(d)** never auto-advance you onward.

## What we won't build

Standing decisions — not "not yet," but "no," unless this document changes:

- **No nudge / recommendation engine.** No "albums you'll like," no algorithmic
  suggestions presented as fact.
- **No automatic resurfacing.** No "on this day you noted X with Y," no memory
  reminders, no people popping up uninvited. Connections are pull-only.
- **No engagement mechanics, no gamification.** No infinite feed, streaks, badges, or
  progress mechanics; no notifications designed to pull you back. A plain mirror you
  *choose* to open (your own counts, the genres you tend to write about) is fine — it
  reflects, it doesn't score. A mirror shows what's there; a mechanic pushes you
  toward more.
- **No doing the wandering for you.** Suggestions, when they exist at all, are
  questions you can ignore, in places you chose to go — never interruptions.

## How to evaluate a feature

1. Does it deepen **story** or enable **connection**?
2. Is it **pull, not push** — does the user reach for it?
3. Does it respect **agency** — can the user ignore it at zero cost, and does it
   strengthen their own discernment rather than substitute for it?
4. Does it keep the **right friction** — is the buy-in part of the value?
5. Is it a **door, not a corridor** — does it preserve the user's place and return at
   zero cost?

If a feature fails 2, 3, 4, or 5, it doesn't belong here, however clever it is.

## Data & privacy

Your notebook — your notes and the links you make — is the one irreplaceable,
user-authored thing in the app. It's kept entirely separate from the catalog, never
touched by a rebuild, and yours to export at any time.

It's **end-to-end encrypted**: encrypted on your device before it's stored or synced,
so wherever it lives — in your browser, or on our server for a hosted account — it's
readable by you and no one else, not even the people who run Music Forest. The only
personal detail we ever hold in the clear is your email.
